Domestic Abuse: Learning the Signs

According to The University of Michigan, abuse can be defined as follows:

Domestic Abuse occurs when a person uses:

  1. physical violence
  2. coercion
  3. threats
  4. intimidation
  5. isolation
  6. stalking
  7. emotional abuse
  8. sexual abuse
  9. economic abuse

University of Michigan Article

"Domestic violence can be a single act or a pattern of behavior in relationships"

“It is a violation of the right we all have to healthy, supportive and safe relationships.”

Survivor

The individual who is being targeted for abuse.

Abuser

The individual who is inflicting the abuse

Signs of physical abuse include:

  1. Black eyes
  2. Bruises on the arms
  3. Busted lips
  4. Red or purple marks on the neck
  5. Sprained wrists

Emotional signs of abuse include:

  1. Agitation, anxiety, or constant apprehension
  2. Changes in sleep habits (sleeping too much or not enough)
  3. Developing a drug or alcohol problem
  4. Extremely apologetic or meek
  5. Loss of interest in daily activities
  6. Low self-esteem
  7. Seeming fearful
  8. Symptoms of Depression
  9. Talking about or referencing to suicide

Behavioral Changes:

  1. Becomes reserved and distant
  2. Begins isolating themselves by cutting off contacts with friends and family members
  3. Cancels appointments or meetings with you at the last minute
  4. Drops out of activities they would usually enjoy
  5. Exhibits excessive privacy concerning their personal life or the person with whom they're in a relationship
  6. Is often late to work or other appointments

Very Well Mind Research

Abusers tend to have common traits and while there isn’t a specific type of “personality” to identify an abuser there are common displays of certain characteristics most abusers have.

Domestic Abuse - Online Chats

Family services

If you are experiencing domestic violence, reaching out for help can be intimidating. For some of us, it's scary to talk about what we're going through out loud. For others, making a call could be a safety risk. If talking online is safer or easier, Family services are here for you. Their trained advocates are now available to chat online to offer information, advocacy and support. You can chat privately with a trained advocate Monday-Friday, 8 a.m.-5 p.m., Pacific Standard Time. Chat is available in Spanish and English.

National Domestic Violence Hotline

This Web site is funded through Grant 2020-V3-GX-0135 from the Office for Victims of Crime, Office of Justice Programs, U.S. Department of Justice. The National Domestic Violence Hotline helps survivors of domestic violence in various langauges. It has a lot of options to get connected, including 24/7 access to resources and support. You can chat with real staff members, and you are able to request to chat to someone in a language that is not English. You can either access their chat by their website, or you have the option to text "START" to 88788 to connect with someone.

Women Against Abuse

We know it’s not always safe or easy to talk by phone when you are seeking resources for an abusive relationship. That’s why WAA(women against abuse) are happy to offer a free, confidential, and anonymous online chat service. When you connect, make sure you are in a safe place and you are using a secure computer that has not been tampered with. You can learn more about their online chat.

Women Helping Women

WHW helps survivors find hope, healing, and empowerment through our confidential support services. They work on preventing Gender_Based Violence and and empowering all survivors. They inspire the community to speak out against gender-based violence, and speak up for equal and healthy relationships. They educate and advocate that generations after us grow into change agents to prevent gender-based violence before it ever starts. You can text 513-381-5610 at any time of day to start a chat. You can also chat with them online.
